Urban Green Spaces

As cities around the world continue to grow, urban planners and public health officials are placing increasing emphasis on the role of green spaces, including parks, gardens, and tree-lined streets, in promoting the well-being of city residents. Research consistently shows that access to nature, even in small amounts, can reduce stress, improve mood, and encourage physical activity.

In many densely populated cities, however, green spaces are unevenly distributed. Wealthier neighborhoods tend to have better-maintained parks and more trees, while lower-income areas often lack adequate green infrastructure. This disparity has led to calls for more equitable urban planning policies that prioritize access for all residents, regardless of income.

Some cities have responded by converting unused lots into community gardens, installing rooftop parks on public buildings, and integrating green corridors into transport routes. These initiatives not only improve air quality and reduce urban heat island effects, but also foster a sense of community among residents who share these spaces.

Critics argue, however, that the costs of developing and maintaining such spaces can be prohibitive for cities with limited budgets, and that funds might be better spent on housing or public transport. Supporters counter that the long-term health benefits and improved quality of life justify the investment.

Part 2: Reading  |  Question 26

The Science of Sleep

Sleep is one of the most fundamental biological processes, yet it remains one of the least understood. Scientists have long known that sleep is essential for good health, but only in recent decades have they begun to unravel the complex mechanisms that govern it.

During sleep, the brain cycles through two main phases: rapid eye movement (REM) sleep and non-REM sleep. Non-REM sleep consists of three stages, from light sleep to deep sleep. It is during deep sleep that the body repairs tissue, builds muscle, and strengthens the immune system. REM sleep, on the other hand, is associated with vivid dreaming and plays a key role in memory consolidation and emotional regulation.

Chronic sleep deprivation has been linked to a wide range of health problems, including obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and depression. Despite this, a significant proportion of the global population regularly fails to get the recommended seven to nine hours of sleep per night. Factors contributing to poor sleep include stress, excessive screen time before bed, irregular schedules, and certain medical conditions such as sleep apnea.

Public health campaigns increasingly emphasize the importance of sleep hygiene, a set of habits and practices that promote consistent, high-quality sleep. These include maintaining a regular sleep schedule, limiting caffeine and alcohol, and creating a cool, dark sleeping environment.
