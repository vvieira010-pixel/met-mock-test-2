Volunteer with Greenvale Community Network

Making a difference, one hour at a time

Who we are
Greenvale Community Network is a local non-profit organisation that connects volunteers with residents who need practical support. We run projects in home assistance, befriending, and neighbourhood improvement across the Greenvale area.

What volunteers do
Depending on availability and preference, volunteers may help older residents with shopping and errands, offer companionship through regular visits or phone calls, assist with community garden maintenance, or support local events. No specialist qualifications are required.

Time commitment
Minimum: Two hours per week
Duration: Rolling, with no fixed end date
Flexibility: Shifts available on weekdays and weekends

How to apply
Complete the short online form at greenvale-network.org/volunteer. A coordinator will contact you within five working days to discuss your interests and arrange a brief induction session.

Q&A: Marta Hendriks, Volunteer Coordinator

Greenvale Community Network

Q: How would you describe the kind of person who typically joins your programme?
A: We get a real mix, including retired professionals, university students, full-time workers who want to give something back. What they share is a wish to feel connected to their neighbourhood. There is no typical volunteer.

Q: What tends to surprise new volunteers?
A: Almost every new volunteer says the same thing: "I didn't expect to feel part of a team so quickly." We pair people thoughtfully, and most form genuine friendships, with residents and with each other.

Q: What is the biggest challenge in running the programme?
A: Capacity. The demand for support from local residents grows every year. We could place twice as many volunteers tomorrow if we had them. That is why outreach matters so much.

Q: What is your outlook for the programme over the next few years?
A: Very positive. Awareness of volunteering is growing, especially among younger adults. I genuinely believe we will see more people deciding that regular community involvement is a normal part of their week, not an unusual thing to do.

C
The Unexpected Benefits of Giving Your Time

Most people who volunteer say they do it to help others. But a growing body of research suggests that the benefits flow in both directions, and regular volunteers may gain as much as the communities they serve.

Studies consistently link volunteering with improved mental health. Volunteers report lower levels of loneliness and higher levels of life satisfaction than non-volunteers. Researchers believe this is partly because volunteering creates a sense of purpose and social connection, both of which are strongly associated with psychological wellbeing. For older adults in particular, the social contact provided by volunteering has been shown to reduce the risk of depression.

There are also physical health associations. Research published in health psychology journals has found that people who volunteer regularly are less likely to develop high blood pressure and tend to live longer than those who do not. While these findings do not prove that volunteering directly causes better health, researchers suggest that the reduced stress, increased activity, and sense of purpose associated with the role may all contribute.

For younger volunteers, there is a practical dimension too. Employers consistently rate qualities such as teamwork, communication, and problem-solving highly, and volunteer work provides genuine opportunities to develop all three. Career advisors increasingly recommend volunteering as a way to build a CV that stands out, particularly for those with limited paid work experience.

Opinion: Why Digital Skills Are No Longer Optional

By Carla Steyn, Adult Education Adviser

Not long ago, it was possible to manage daily life comfortably without using a computer. Today, that is no longer the case for most people. Booking a doctor's appointment, applying for a job, managing a bank account, and renewing a driving licence all increasingly require digital access. For anyone who lacks the basic skills to navigate these systems, ordinary tasks become unnecessarily difficult, expensive, or time-consuming.

I understand why many adults feel hesitant. Learning new technology can feel intimidating, particularly if past attempts have left people feeling foolish or lost. But in my experience of working in adult education for over fifteen years, I have never met someone who genuinely could not learn these skills. I have only met people who had not yet had the right support to do so. The barrier is almost always confidence, not ability.

What is needed is more investment in accessible, jargon-free training delivered by patient, qualified instructors, as well as wider recognition that digital skills are now as fundamental as reading and writing. Treating digital literacy as a "nice to have" is no longer defensible when so much of modern life depends on it.

C
Digital Skills and the Workforce: What the Evidence Shows

Research consistently shows that digital skills are among the most in-demand competencies in today's labour market. A 2023 survey of over 2,000 UK employers found that 82 per cent rated digital literacy as important or very important when hiring, yet more than half reported difficulty finding applicants with adequate skills. This gap has significant consequences, both for workers who struggle to access employment and for businesses that cannot find the staff they need.

Workers who develop digital skills are not only more likely to be employed, but tend to earn higher wages and have greater job security. A study tracking adults who completed accredited digital training found that within one year of finishing a course, 68 per cent had moved into new roles or secured a promotion. Among those who had been unemployed at the time of enrolment, nearly half had found work within six months.

From the employer's perspective, investing in staff digital training also delivers measurable returns. Companies that fund training internally report improvements in productivity, a reduction in errors associated with manual processes, and greater staff retention, since employees who receive training tend to feel more valued and are less likely to leave. For small businesses in particular, the efficiency gains from even basic improvements in how staff use digital tools can be significant.

Researchers conclude that closing the digital skills gap requires action at multiple levels: affordable training accessible to all adults, employer commitment to funding continuing development, and recognition in policy that digital literacy is a core public good, not simply a concern for the technology sector.
