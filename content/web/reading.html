Reading Passage 1 — Urban Farming

Urban farming has become increasingly popular in cities around the world. From rooftop gardens to community plots, city residents are finding creative ways to grow their own food. Proponents argue that urban agriculture offers numerous benefits, including improved access to fresh produce, reduced transportation costs, and stronger community bonds. Critics, however, point to challenges such as soil contamination, limited space, and the relatively high cost of urban land. Despite these obstacles, many cities have begun incorporating urban farming into their planning policies. In Detroit, for example, vacant lots have been transformed into productive gardens that supply local farmers markets. Similarly, Singapore has integrated vertical farming into its urban landscape, using innovative technology to maximize food production in limited spaces. These initiatives not only provide fresh food but also create green spaces that improve air quality and reduce the urban heat island effect. As cities continue to grow, urban farming may become an essential component of sustainable urban development.

Reading Passage 2 — The Science of Habit Formation

Habits are automatic behaviors triggered by specific cues in our environment. According to research by MIT neuroscientists, the brain's basal ganglia plays a central role in habit formation through a three-step loop: cue, routine, and reward. The cue triggers the behavior, the routine is the behavior itself, and the reward is the positive outcome that reinforces the loop. Understanding this cycle is key to changing unwanted habits. Charles Duhigg, author of The Power of Habit, suggests that rather than trying to eliminate a bad habit entirely, we should focus on changing the routine while keeping the same cue and reward. For example, if stress (cue) leads to eating junk food (routine) because it provides comfort (reward), replacing the routine with a short walk or meditation can transform the habit while still satisfying the underlying need. Research also shows that habit strength depends on context consistency. Performing a behavior in the same setting each time strengthens the cue-routine association. A study published in the European Journal of Social Psychology found that it takes an average of 66 days for a new behavior to become automatic, though this varies widely depending on the complexity of the behavior and individual differences.
